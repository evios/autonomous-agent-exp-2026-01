## Content Templates (Ready to Use)

### Template 1: Founder Evolution

**Structure:**
1. Hook: [Role A] → [Role B] timeline
2. What I thought vs. what I learned
3. Specific example/story
4. Reframe: What this means for others
5. CTA: What would you add?

**Example:**
"Infrastructure Engineer (2006) → Startup Founder (2011) → AI Product Leader (2021).

15 years. Two companies. Here's what I was most wrong about:

**I thought:** Hiring is about finding smart people.
**I learned:** Hiring is about finding people who thrive in chaos.

OSIS (2011-2018): Hired brilliant engineers who wanted clear requirements.
Result: Constant friction. They waited for specs. Startups don't have specs.

Ender Turing (2018-now): Hired people who ask 'what problem are we solving?' not 'what's the spec?'
Result: They ship. They iterate. They own outcomes.

The shift: From 'execute my plan' to 'help me figure out the plan.'

In infrastructure, clarity was the job. In startups, ambiguity is the job.

What's one thing you wish you knew before your first hire?"

### Template 2: Scaling Reality Check

**Structure:**
1. Hook: [Failure stat] + [Bold claim]
2. Why this happens (common mistake)
3. What actually works (from experience)
4. Specific tactic/framework
5. CTA: Link or engagement question

**Example:**
"70% of startups fail at scaling (not at launch). After scaling two companies, here's why:

**The trap:** Unstructured workflows worked at 10 people, so founders ignore them at 50.
**The reality:** What breaks at 50: decision-making, communication, accountability.

What worked for us (OSIS → Ender Turing):

→ Document processes while they're still simple (10-15 people)
→ Hire middle layer early (not just doers, process designers)
→ Kill 'founder approval' bottleneck (train people to make decisions)
→ Weekly metrics reviews (what gets measured gets improved)

Scaling isn't 'do more of what worked.' It's 'build the machine that does what you used to do.'

Most founders realize this at 40 people. By then, it's a reorg, not a build.

Start at 15. Thank me at 50."

### Template 3: Path Wisdom

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Unexpected career path stated
2. Why this path seemed wrong/unusual
3. What connected the dots
4. Valuable lesson from the combination
5. CTA: Encourage unconventional paths

**Example:**
"5 years designing enterprise networks.
7 years running a tech startup.
5+ years building AI products.

Weird path? Maybe. But here's what designing LAN/WAN architectures taught me about building startups:

**Infrastructure thinking:**
- Plan for 10x scale from day one
- Single points of failure kill systems
- Redundancy costs upfront, saves millions later
- Complexity is the enemy (KISS principle)

**Startup application:**
- Hire for 2 stages ahead, not just today
- Founder dependencies = SPOF (train others to decide)
- Build financial runway early (12-18 months minimum)
- Simple business models beat complex ones

The weirdest part? I thought infrastructure skills wouldn't transfer.

Turns out: Systems thinking is systems thinking. Networks, teams, products—same principles.

Your unconventional background isn't a liability. It's differentiation.

What's yours?"

### Template 4: Fundraising Reality

**Structure:**
1. Hook: Common fundraising mistake
2. Why this kills momentum
3. What readiness actually means
4. Checklist/framework
5. CTA: DM for deck template (engagement)

**Example:**
"Your fundraising dies in the follow-up.

Intro → great first call → investor asks for deck/metrics → you need a week to pull it together → momentum gone.

After raising for two companies (Ukraine + Estonia), here's the 48-hour founder kit:

**Always ready:**
→ Investor deck (10-12 slides, updated monthly)
→ Financial model (3 scenarios: conservative/base/optimistic)
→ 1-pager (problem/solution/traction/ask)
→ Data room link (cap table, contracts, metrics dashboard)

**Why this matters:**
Fundraising is sales. Friction kills deals. Fast follow-up = serious founder.

**Bonus:** Preparing this forces clarity. If you can't explain your business in 12 slides, you don't understand it yet.

Build the kit before you need it. When the warm intro comes, you're ready.

Preparation isn't paranoia. It's competitive advantage.

(DM me if you want the deck template we used. Built for product founders, not MBAs.)"

### Template 5: AI Moat Defense

**Structure:**
1. Hook: The question every AI founder hears
2. Why this question matters (market reality)
3. Real answer from Ender Turing
4. Framework: 3 types of defensibility
5. CTA: What's your moat?

**Example:**
"Every AI founder hears this: 'What prevents OpenAI from copying you in 6 months?'

If you don't have a crisp answer, you don't have a business.

**Our answer (Ender Turing, call center AI):**
7 years of proprietary call center data. 10M+ analyzed calls. Domain-specific ASR models trained on real customer conversations.

OpenAI has generalist models. We have specialist expertise.

**The 3 AI moats that work in 2026:**

1. **Proprietary data** (we have this)
   - Years of domain-specific data
   - Feedback loops that improve with scale
   - Data network effects

2. **Vertical integration** (e.g., hardware + software)
   - Hard to replicate full stack
   - Switching costs for customers

3. **Domain expertise** (deep knowledge + relationships)
   - 7 years in call centers
   - We understand the workflow, not just the tech
   - Trusted by banking/insurance/healthcare

**Generic AI wrappers = dead.**
**Domain AI with defensibility = fundable.**

What's your moat?"
